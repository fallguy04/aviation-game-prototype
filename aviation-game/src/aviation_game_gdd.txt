An Aviation Corporate Strategy Board Game
Version 1.0  |  Design Draft


1. Game Overview
1.1 Core Philosophy
This is a cutthroat corporate strategy board game set in the North American aviation industry. The central tension is between institutional health and personal enrichment. Players are not simply building airlines — they are exploiting them.

Win Condition
Highest Personal Net Worth at game end. Net Worth = Liquid Cash + Total Stock Value across all holdings. The player who runs the biggest airline does not necessarily win. The player who extracts the most wealth does.


1.2 The Mammoth Trap
Running a massive airline is a burden, not a guaranteed path to victory. Overhead scales aggressively with network size. The truly dangerous player is often the one sitting quietly with diversified holdings across multiple airlines, collecting dividends while CEOs compete to see who can build the most expensive liability.
1.3 Player Count & Play Time

Target play time: 90 minutes.

2. Design Aesthetic
High-Fidelity Essentialist — no unnecessary complexity in components or rules
Unbranded color palettes (e.g., Navy/Red, Canyon Blue/Yellow) represent real airlines without IP issues
Minimalist wooden sticks for routes, acrylic cubes for demand, satisfying tactile pegboards for the corporate dashboard
All treasury values are fully public — open cash trays on the corporate dashboard at all times

3. Board Architecture & Components
3.1 The Map
North American map featuring strict predefined Trunk and Spoke printed route lines. The board begins in a predetermined scenario setup (similar to Axis & Allies), so players step into a living world immediately — existing partial route networks, stacked demand cubes, and ghost airlines already operating.

Route Slots
Every printed route line has exactly two slots: Primary (Top/Left) and Secondary (Bottom/Right)
Route sticks are placed in these slots to indicate airline occupancy
Both slots occupied by different airlines triggers a Fare War

Demand Cubes

4. The Stock Market & Capital Flow
4.1 Stock Price Formula

Stock Price Formula
Stock Price = Total Routes + (Total Hubs x 3)This formula is 100% transparent. Every player can calculate any airline's stock price at any time by looking at the board.


4.2 Buying & Selling Stock
Shares are unlimited — any player may buy any number of shares in any airline at any time during the stock phase
When a player buys shares, cash goes DIRECTLY into the Airline's Treasury
When a player sells shares, cash comes from the Bank — the airline treasury is untouched
No player may be prevented from buying or selling shares in any airline
Shares can be used as investment, dilution instruments, takeover weapons, or alliance signals

The Dilution Dynamic
Every share purchase simultaneously does four things:
Invests capital into the airline (treasury funded)
Dilutes every existing shareholder's future dividend per share
Moves the buyer one step closer to the 5-share CEO threshold
Signals intent that every other player can read publicly

Hostile Share Accumulation
An enemy buying into your airline is a weapon. If your airline has 10 shares outstanding and pays $2/share dividends, a hostile player buying 5 more shares cuts every existing shareholder's payout by one third. No vote required.

4.3 CEO Control
The player with the most shares in an airline, minimum 5, controls that airline as CEO
A player may hold the CEO chair in multiple airlines simultaneously
CEO turns require operational decisions — multiple CEOs means more decisions per round

4.4 Ghost Fleets (Autopilot Airlines)
Airlines without a CEO (fewer than 5 shares held by any single player) run on autopilot.
Ghost airlines automatically pay a flat $1/share dividend from the Bank each round
Ghost airlines have no live treasury and cannot expand or change routes
The predetermined board setup creates multiple ghost fleets at game start, creating immediate investor decisions
Ghost fleet routes remain on the board and can trigger fare wars with live airlines

Why Ghost Fleets Matter
Ghost fleets are passive income machines. A player collecting $1/share from 3 ghost airlines while others fight fare wars and fuel spikes can quietly accumulate the winning net worth. But ghost airlines are also sitting ducks — their fixed routes and autopilot behavior make them predictable targets for fare war harassment.


5. CEO Operations — The Turn Sequence
5.1 Complete Turn Structure

Critical Timing Note
Revenue is collected BEFORE routes are placed, but expenses are paid AFTER — INCLUDING on newly placed routes. Every expansion is a bet made with last round's money against next round's costs. There is no grace period.


Phase 1 — Player Stock Market (Round Robin)
All players buy and sell stock round-robin, one stock can be bought or sold per turn. When all players pass out of the stock purchasing phase, proceed to the Airlines Phase.

Phase 2 — Airlines Phase
Collect Revenue — base route revenue + hub bonuses, minus fare war penalties. Stock purchase cash enters treasury asynchronously as purchases occur.
Plan Routes — round robin, add or remove one route per turn. Declare any M&A actions. When all pass, route planning ends.
Pay Expenses — fuel costs on ALL current routes (including newly placed this phase), hub taxes at progressive rates.
Resolve Bankruptcies — any airline that cannot pay expenses enters bankruptcy proceedings.
CEO Dividend Declarations — each CEO declares dividend payout per share, cash leaves treasury to investors.

Phase 3 — Event & Admin
Draw Era event cards
Set new fuel price
Perform CEO evaluation (share counts checked — control may change)
Check end-of-game triggers

5.2 Revenue Collection Detail
Base Revenue
Sum of all Demand Cubes connected by the airline's routes
Hub Bonus
Routes touching a Hub city generate an automatic +$2 bonus (simulates connecting traffic)
Hubs entrench an airline in high-traffic markets and meaningfully inflate stock price via the formula
Fare War Penalty
Triggered when two different airlines occupy both the Primary and Secondary slots on the same route
Both airlines suffer a flat -$2 revenue penalty for that route
Fare wars function as a deterrent, not just a punishment — a contested route is often worth less than an uncontested regional one
Small airlines with 4-5 routes can surgically contest the most profitable routes of a giant, imposing a cost on dominance without needing to match its scale

5.3 Route Management
Placing Routes
Route sticks are FREE to add or remove — no placement cost
The true cost is operational overhead paid at end of phase
This makes route management a risk calculus, not a resource gate
Building Hubs
Costs $15 from the Treasury
Grants exactly 2 free route placements instantly upon construction
Maximum 1 Hub per city
Hub tax is progressive and escalates with network size (see Expenses)
Hubs raise stock price by +3 per hub via the stock formula, making them entrenchment tools

Fuel Price Range (Design Target)
Base fuel: ~$2-4 per route. Spike fuel: up to $6 per route. An airline with 15 routes swings from $30-60 to $90 in expenses during a spike. Lean airlines survive. Overextended airlines collapse. This is intentional.


5.5 Dividend Declarations
CEO simply declares a per-share payout amount (e.g., '$2 per share')
Cash leaves the Treasury and goes directly to all shareholders proportionally
No division math required — CEO names the rate, multiply by shares held
Dividend hoarding creates risk: treasury accumulates, making the airline an attractive takeover target
Refusing dividends while other investors collect income elsewhere puts a visible target on the CEO's back

6. Bankruptcy & The Phoenix Mechanic
6.1 Bankruptcy Trigger
If a Treasury cannot cover total expenses, the airline enters bankruptcy
The airline is wiped from the board — all routes and hubs removed
Stock price drops to $0
All shareholders of the bankrupt airline receive $1 per share conciliation payment from the Bank
Players retain their personal cash and all other stock holdings — no player is eliminated

Key Design Principle
Players are permanently independent from any single airline. A CEO whose airline collapses is immediately back to being a pure investor. They may quietly accumulate shares in the airline that just absorbed their market share.


6.2 The Phoenix Mechanic — Relaunching a Bankrupt Airline
During Phase 1 (Stock Market) of the following round, any bankrupt airline may be relaunched via silent auction.

Auction Process
Any player may bid to relaunch a bankrupt airline
All interested players write a secret bid (minimum $10)
Bids are revealed simultaneously
Highest bidder wins — their bid goes DIRECTLY into the airline's new treasury
Winner receives 3 shares in the airline and the CEO chair
Airline enters service in Phase 2 of the same round

No Relaunch
Players may collectively opt not to relaunch an airline
The route slots on the map return to empty and become available territory

7. Hostile Takeovers (M&A)
7.1 Overview
A CEO can use their airline's War Chest to launch a formal 3-round acquisition siege against a rival airline. This is announced publicly and is binding once declared.

7.2 Takeover Sequence
ANNOUNCE: Attacking CEO formally declares the target airline during Route Planning phase
FREEZE: Attacker has 3 rounds to accumulate cash in their treasury equal to the Target's Total Market Cap (Stock Price x Outstanding Shares)
DEFEND: Target CEO may raise their stock price by adding routes and hubs — making the buyout more expensive. However, this burns treasury on overhead.
RESOLVE: At the end of round 3, compare attacker treasury to target market cap

Resolution — Success
Attacker absorbs all routes and hubs of the target airline
Redundant hubs (same city) are liquidated for a cash bonus
Target shareholders receive a payout at the final market cap price per share
Target airline ceases to exist as a separate entity
Resolution — Failure
Attacker pays a flat $15 Legal/Admin penalty from their Treasury to the Bank
No other consequence — the siege simply ends

Bluff Tax Design Note
The $15 penalty goes to the Bank, not the defender. This prevents defenders from being incentivized to encourage harassment sieges just to farm the penalty. The penalty creates genuine commitment cost without weaponizing it.


7.3 Collusion & Table Politics
No agreements are binding. All deals are enforced only by reputation and self-interest. This is intentional.

Players holding shares in two airlines are natural brokers between their CEOs
A CEO may pay dividends specifically to maintain the loyalty of a pivotal investor
Fare war contestation is the visible signal that an alliance has ended
Kingmaking (a trailing player deciding the winner by their share sales) is legal, expected, and should be treated as a feature of the game, not a bug
Table talk is always legal — no information is secret beyond personal cash holdings and bid amounts in silent auctions

8. Player Archetypes & Strategic Depth
8.1 The Four Archetypes
These are not rigid roles but a strategic spectrum. A player may shift archetype mid-game as conditions change.

The Pure Investor
Never holds the CEO chair — or holds it briefly and deliberately exits
Diversifies across 3-5 airlines, collecting dividends from each
Controls nothing directly but is indispensable to every CEO who needs loyal majority shareholders
Weakness: entirely dependent on CEOs not mismanaging their airlines
Counter: patient, disciplined, and the hardest archetype to identify as a threat until it is too late

The Cash Cow CEO
Builds one dominant airline, entrench hubs, pumps stock price
Extracts wealth via self-dividends — controls when and how much to pay
Strength: maximum control over a single powerful asset
Weakness: overhead scales brutally, fare wars become unavoidable at scale, becomes the most attractive M&A target on the board
The Mammoth Trap: the tallest tree attracts the most lightning

The Long-Game CEO
Builds efficiently with an eye on sustainable overhead rather than maximum expansion
Delays extraction in favor of stock price growth — plans to cash out at the right moment
The Long-Game CEO becomes the Cash Cow CEO through pure mechanical pressure once the treasury grows fat enough
Weakness: other investors may accumulate shares and challenge for the CEO chair before the patient plan matures

The Startup Raider
Runs lean, often late-game airlines with minimal overhead
Deliberately contests the most profitable routes of giants to suppress their revenue via fare wars
Accumulates shares in wounded giants while they are financially distressed
Either triggers a bankruptcy to absorb them cheaply or mounts a formal takeover
Weakness: $15 earnest money on failed takeovers; 3-round siege window requires precise timing

The Rock-Paper-Scissors Dynamic
Pure Investor needs Cash Cow CEO to pay dividends. Cash Cow CEO needs Startup Raider to not exist. Startup Raider needs Pure Investor to not hold enough shares to defend the giant being targeted. No single archetype dominates — optimal play shifts with the board state.


8.2 The Institutional Decay Arc
The most thematically rich dynamic in the game: a CEO who begins as Long-Game inevitably faces the mechanical temptation to extract. The treasury grows. Other investors are collecting dividends from other airlines. The CEO's own shares are illiquid. The shift from Long-Game to Cash Cow to collapse is not a moral failing — it is the natural outcome of the mechanics, and it mirrors real corporate history with uncomfortable accuracy.

9. Eras & Scenario Design
9.1 Overview
The game features 3 distinct era decks, each a separate scenario with unique board setup, varying ghost fleets, and distinct macroeconomic event cards. Each era creates a different game feel and rewards different strategies.

1950s Golden Age
High demand across major corridors, low base fuel costs
Large ghost fleets — aviation is new, glamorous, and undercontrolled
Game is expansionist and optimistic early; consolidation hits as event cards progress
Sample events: Jet Age arrival (route value shift), government regulation, international route auctions

1980s Deregulation
High fuel volatility — spikes frequent and severe
New routes opening constantly, fare wars endemic from early rounds
Bankruptcy common — the Phoenix mechanic fires frequently
Recommended for experienced players — maximum chaos, maximum drama
Sample events: sudden deregulation (route slot expansion), fuel crisis, mega-merger rumors

2010s Consolidation
Fewer ghost fleets — industry already concentrated at game start
Massive predetermined route networks with high overhead built in
M&A is the dominant mechanic — formal takeovers fire multiple times per game
Game is slower, more calculated; stock price end triggers fire faster
Investor archetypes dominate — CEO roles are high-stakes and high-pressure

Era Deck Pacing Principle
First 2-3 event cards in each era should be mild — let the game find its footing before volatility spikes. Front-loaded chaos punishes new players in ways that feel unfair rather than instructive. Volatility should escalate as the game matures.


10. End Game Triggers & Final Scoring
10.1 Acquire-Style Triggers
The game ends instantly when either of the following conditions is met:

Trigger
Condition
Trigger A
One single airline reaches a Stock Price of $40 or higher
Trigger B
Three different airlines each reach a Stock Price of $25 or higher


10.2 Final Scoring

Final Score Formula
Personal Net Worth = Liquid Cash in Hand + (Shares Owned x Current Stock Price per airline, summed across all holdings)The player with the highest Net Worth wins.


11. Map Design Guidelines
11.1 Density Targets
Total route slots: approximately 120-160 across the full map
Enough for 6-8 airlines to each hold 12-18 routes without total saturation
Two airlines building aggressively should begin crowding each other; three should trigger endemic fare wars
Empty route slots are the norm — economic viability, not map coverage, determines where airlines operate

11.2 Trunk vs. Spoke Geometry
Trunks: high-revenue contested corridors (Chicago-New York, Dallas-LA, Atlanta-Miami) — the battleground routes
Spokes: quieter feeders that become valuable only when connected to an entrenched hub
The map encodes strategic geography — players intuitively understand why Atlanta is always contested

11.3 Scenario Setup
Each era scenario specifies starting route placements, demand cube distributions, and ghost fleet compositions
Setup is predetermined — players read a living world, not a blank slate
This creates immediate round-1 strategic decisions: which ghost airline is undervalued? Where is demand highest?

14.3 ERA SETUP — GHOST FLEET CONFIGURATIONS
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Ghost Fleet dividends are set by Era Setup Cards, NOT a flat rate.
D6 Volatility Roll applies to ALL Ghost Fleet dividend payouts.

  Roll 1-3: $0 payout
  Roll 4-5: Base dividend
  Roll 6:   Base dividend + $1 bonus

GHOST FLEET ARCHETYPES (used in scenario setup cards):

Type         | Base Div | Notes
-------------|----------|------------------------------------------
Legacy Bloat | $2/share | Large route network, high overhead risk
  Established | $1/share | Stable mid-size airline, safe investment
Startup Lean | $1/share | Small network, low overhead, growth upside
Falling Knife| $0/share | Near-bankruptcy, zero passive return,
             |          | attractive only as M&A/takeover target

M&A THRESHOLD FOR GHOST FLEETS:
  Market Cap for buyout = Stock Price x 5 (fixed share count proxy)
  (Ghost fleets have no issued share dial — use 5 as the constant)

14.4 M&A RULES (UPDATED)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- M&A failure penalty: $20 flat fee to Bank (not $15)
- Hubs CANNOT be liquidated for cash under any circumstance,
  including post-takeover redundant hubs. Abandoning a hub
  removes tax liability only. No cash recovery.
- Post-takeover redundant hubs are abandoned (not liquidated).
  The only cash gains in the game are:
    1. Route revenue collection
    2. Investor share purchases entering treasury

14.6 DESIGN NOTES ON THE MAP DATA
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- ATL, ORD, DFW, JFK are the four most-connected nodes by design.
  These will be the natural flashpoints for fare wars and hub
  entrenchment. Expect 80% of M&A activity to orbit these cities.

- The West Coast triangle (LAX-SFO-SEA) is intentionally dense —
  3 high-demand routes in a tight cluster rewards a focused
  West Coast airline archetype.

- The TX interior cluster (DFW-IAH-SAT-AUS) creates a regional
  battleground that rewards a "Southwest Airlines" style lean
  operator with tight geographic focus.

- Canada nodes (YYZ, YVR) are peripheral but provide low-
  competition routes for patient investors building ghost fleets
  or startup airlines away from the main fare war zones.

- Low-demand Regionals (CMH, PIT, IND, ABQ interior routes) are
  economically marginal at base fuel price. They become valuable
  when: (a) fuel drops, (b) a hub is built nearby, or (c) a player
  wants to contest a rival's spoke traffic cheaply.
